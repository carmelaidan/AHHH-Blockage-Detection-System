THE PROBLEM AND LITERATURE REVIEW
This chapter establishes the foundation for this study. It begins with an
introduction that provides background information and context, followed by the
theoretical and conceptual frameworks guiding the research. The chapter then
presents the statement of the problem, outlining the specific research questions
and hypotheses. The scope and delimitations clearly define the study's
boundaries, while the limitations address potential constraints. Finally, the
significance of the study emphasizes its contributions to the field, and a
comprehensive definition of terms ensures clarity and consistency throughout the
research.
Background of the Study
Urban flooding is a growing global crisis. It results from rapid urbanization
and climate change, threatening infrastructure and public safety (Rentschler et al.,
2022; Cariaso, 2025). Cities like Jakarta and Mumbai suffer major economic losses
due to poor drainage systems (Asdak et al., 2018; New Media, 2024). In 2023,
floods affected over 50 million people, leading to contaminated water, sanitation
disruptions, and a 20% increase in diarrheal disease cases (Acosta-Espa√±a et al.,
2024). With an average of 19 to 20 typhoons each year (Espiritu, 2024), the
Philippines faces challenges from clogged drains and slow inspections (Ombay &
Locus, 2024). The severe effects of these conditions were evident during Typhoon

Ondoy, which cost the government around $4.42 billion (GFDRR, 2022).
Moreover, Metro Manila's aging and silted drainage system (Punzalan & Dizon,
2025) is insufficient, according to DPWH Secretary Manuel Bonoan, and suffers
from substandard or nonexistent flood control projects (Sadongdong, 2025;
Superable, 2025). This situation shows a pressing need for community-level early
warning systems and real-time monitoring for drainage management.
The Internet of Things (IoT) has great potential to improve flood and
drainage management. It includes pollution monitoring (Zulkifli et al., 2022) and
open GIS data for urban drainage planning (See et al., 2020). Abedin and Stephen
(2019) developed a GIS-based model that produced flood-extent maps showing
possible locations and severity. Acoustic reflectometry achieved 95% accuracy in
blockage detection (Kelly et al., 2024). Te et al. (2024) used pressure sensors,
LoRaWAN, and solar power, while Rajput et al. (2025) used ultrasonic sensors
and GSM modules. While these studies propose detection methodologies, there is
a distinct lack of published research providing a rigorous, quantitative performance
comparison of such novel detection systems against established benchmarks or
prior art. Therefore, this study will aim to address this critical gap by conducting a
comparative performance evaluation of the proposed Automated Hydro-Hazard
Helper (AHHH) Blockage Detection System against the standardized Rajput et al.
(2025) logic, specifically assessing its practicality, durability, and effectiveness
using verified quantitative metrics.

Urban Drainage Early Warning System
The development and implementation of effective early warning systems for
urban flooding and drainage management are increasingly recognized as critical
priorities. Recent studies highlight innovative approaches for real-time

Recall and Precision
In Bramer, Giustini, and Kramer‚Äôs (2016) study, they evaluated the recall
and precision of the three leading databases: Embase, MEDLINE, and Google
Scholar, by comparing the databases with respect to their effectiveness for
systematic reviews. The combination of Embase and MEDLINE was found to
achieve the highest recall (81.6%), while still maintaining low precision (‚âà2%).
Broad searches were thus demonstrated to result in high completeness, but at the
same time, to entail a larger number of irrelevancies.
On the other hand, Sadeli and Lawanda (2023) focused on recall, precision,
and F-measure in an Electronic Document Management System (EDMS) and
found almost perfect recall (1.00) and high precision (0.9713) when the search
syntax was optimized, for example, with the use of quotation marks. Integrating
both studies, recall guarantees that full retrieval and precision give better
relevance; thus, the trade-off between them depends on the system‚Äôs objective,
either for thorough academic searches or quick document management. For the
AHHH Blockage Detection System, high recall ensures that all actual blockages
are detected (minimizing false negatives), while high precision ensures that alerts

Alert Response Time and Data Rate
Alert response time and data rate have been particularly highlighted in the
context of the integration of IoT for early warning and public safety systems. A case
in point is the systematic review of IoT-based early warning systems for floods,
earthquakes, tsunamis, and landslides by Esposito et al. (2022).
Recent studies have taken the latency issue of time-critical IoT systems
even further. According to Leclerc et al. (2025), achieving a high service level
requires minimizing both transmission delays and processing times. At the same
time, Zhang, Zhang, and Sun (2025) worked on a prototype IoT-based public
safety alert system that can deliver alerts in less than 450 milliseconds. Their
findings underscore the fact that the design decisions made regarding protocol,
network type, and edge processing can significantly impact the latency
experienced in realistic emergencies.
Complementing these latency-focused studies, recent work has also
emphasized the importance of data rate in ensuring reliable sensor communication
in environmental monitoring systems. Rahman et al. (2022) evaluated how packet
size, channel-listening intervals, and network setup affect transmission
performance. At the same time, Figueiredo et al. (2019) introduced RiverCore, a
cellular-based IoT device for water-level monitoring.

Theoretical Framework
The foundation of this study is based on two theories, Modified Manning‚Äôs
Equation and Systems Theory. Together, these theories form the hydraulic and
structural foundation for the IoT- and GIS-based AHHH Blockage Detection
System for storm drains.
The Modified Manning's Equation, first proposed by Robert Manning in 1891
and later extended by studies like Wang et al. 2024, is the basis for understanding
flow behavior in open-channel systems, including storm drains. This research will
measure the effects of obstructions on flow movement, hydraulic radius, and water
level, key indicators of blockages.

The study is then anchored in the Systems Theory (Ludwig von Bertalanffy,
1968), which establishes the structure and functionality of the proposed system.
Systems Theory has been used in various engineering studies to establish the
design of automated systems that are interconnected and dynamic with self-
regulating capabilities and feedback mechanisms, including autonomous control
systems, smart monitoring technology, and process automation (Fortuna &
Buscarino, 2024). Likewise, the study leverages Systems Theory as it relates to
the interconnectivity and coordination among the sensing, processing, and
communication components of the AHHH Blockage Detection System.
Systems Theory provides a direct line of connection to the dependent
variables or performance metrics by facilitating the effective exchange of data and
information, thereby allowing for the quickest response time built into the system.
In conclusion, Systems Theory supports the engineering basis for the study by
allowing each system subsystem to work in harmony to establish sustainable
operational efficiency and reliability.

Conceptual Framework
The research is built upon a conceptual framework primarily rooted in the
Modified Manning‚Äôs Equation. The equation serves as the fundamental basis for
understanding hydraulic principles and informing system design. This equation
guides the selection of sensors, hardware configuration, data requirements, and
testing procedures. Systems Theory provides a strong foundation for organizing
and integrating the various subsystems involved in blockage detection. This theory
facilitates the design and assessment of two key prototype systems: the
experimental AHHH Blockage Detection System and the Standardized Hardware
Benchmark System (SHBS), adapted from Rajput et al. (2025), which serves as a
comparative benchmark.
To enhance system capabilities, the framework integrates Internet of Things
(IoT) and Geographic Information Systems (GIS) technologies. IoT enables
continuous, remote monitoring and data collection, while GIS provides spatial
visualization and mapping of blockage occurrences. The AHHH system leverages
both IoT and GIS, while the SHBS primarily relies on IoT with SMS alerts. The
performance of both systems is rigorously evaluated under controlled laboratory
conditions, simulating real-world scenarios. Key performance metrics include recall
score, precision, data rate, electrical power consumption, and alert response time.
Statistical analysis, using descriptive statistics and paired t-tests, will be employed
to determine significant performance differences between the systems.

Scope, Delimitations, and Limitations
This study develops and assesses an IoT and GIS-based early warning
system for storm drain blockages, addressing the current lack of effective, real-
18

========== PAGE 20 ==========
time monitoring that contributes to increased urban flooding. To combat this issue,
the study will define the IoT sensors, GIS technology, system designs, and
performance metrics required for blockage detection, and develop and
comparatively evaluate two system designs against conventional methods. By
providing a more effective means of early detection and response, this research
aims to enhance urban resilience and mitigate flood risks.
This study evaluates a blockage detection prototype's performance,
focusing on key indicators: recall score, precision, data rate, electrical power
consumption, and alert response time, assessed via individual t-tests. Detection
efficacy, measured as recall and precision scores, assesses the system's ability to
correctly identify blockages while minimizing both false negatives (recall) and false
positives (precision). Beyond accuracy, electrical power consumption indicates the
system's energy efficiency and reflects its operational sustainability. Data
communication is assessed by the data rate (bits/s), which measures the speed at
which sensor readings are sent to the central unit. Finally, alert response time
(seconds) indicates how quickly blockages trigger notifications, gauging
responsiveness. Statistical significance is evaluated using individual t-tests to
ensure the reliability of the results.

design will be employed, comparing a fully functional hardware prototype
